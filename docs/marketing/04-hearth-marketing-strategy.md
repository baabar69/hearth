The single document that should govern every marketing decision until it is replaced by a newer version of itself.

**Version:** 2026-04

## 1. The product, in one paragraph

Hearth is a peer-support membership. Members are paired with a Keeper — a trained, paid companion (not a licensed clinician) — for biweekly or weekly Sits, an asynchronous Long Talk thread, and group Circles. $39/month for the biweekly tier (Hearthside), $99/month for the weekly tier (Hearth Deep). Open to members worldwide. Keepers are based primarily in South Asia and bring deep cultural fluency for those who want it. The promise: *"Pull up a chair. You weren't meant to carry it alone."*

## 2. Positioning

### The category problem

Hearth has no clean analog. It is not therapy. It is not coaching. It is not a meditation app. It is not a friendship app. We use April Dunford's positioning method to choose where Hearth stands when a stranger sees it for the first time.

### Competitive alternatives (what members do if Hearth doesn't exist)

| Alternative | Strength | Weakness Hearth exploits |
|---|---|---|
| Therapy | Clinical credibility | $200/session, cold, intermittent, no cultural fluency |
| Calling a friend | Free, familiar | Friends get tired, are part of the weight, can't hold weeks |
| Meditation app | Cheap, low-friction | One-way, silent, no listener |
| AI companion (Replika, Pi) | 24/7, cheap | Not human, no real witness, doesn't notice silence |
| Drop-in peer chat (7 Cups) | Free | Anonymous, rotating, no relationship |
| 12-week group programs | Curriculum, community | Time-bound, group, founder-as-brand |
| Nothing (the default) | Costs nothing | Costs everything, slowly |

### How we position

**Frame of reference (the room Hearth walks into):** *peer support for the long term.* Not a sub-category of therapy. Not a sub-category of coaching. A category of its own — closest in spirit to the elder, the auntie, the cousin who'd been through it.

**One-sentence positioning statement:**
> *For anyone carrying weight that doesn't fit a couch, Hearth is the trained companion you stay paired with — not therapy, not a chatbot, not a friend — for as long as it takes.*

**Three positioning planks** (every piece of marketing should sit on one of these three):
1. **Paired** — one human, the same person every week, for as long as it takes.
2. **Slow** — built for the months and years, not the 10-minute reset.
3. **Yours** — cultural fluency, language fluency, in your time zone, on your terms.

### What we are NOT

- We are not therapy. Say it plainly. Repeat it.
- We are not a crisis service.
- We are not "BetterHelp but cheaper."
- We are not optimised for engagement, streaks, or daily logins.
- We do not run urgency-carnival sales (no "expires tonight!" scarcity).

This list is positioning, not legal disclaimer. We market the *non-things* loudly because the field is crowded with the things we are not.

## 3. Audience

### The Smallest Viable Audience (SVA)

Following Godin: we name *one* archetypal person and write everything for her, knowing her delight will spread to people like her.

**Primary SVA — "the eldest daughter who has been the family's emotional infrastructure for 15 years."**

She is 28–45. Educated, employed (often in caregiving-heavy professions: medicine, teaching, design, nonprofits). Often a partner; sometimes a mother. She is the person everyone calls. She has tried therapy, journaled for years, listened to every podcast. She is not in crisis. She is *quietly tired in a way she can't explain to anyone in her life without hurting them.*

She doesn't need to be saved. She needs a witness.

### Secondary SVAs (acquire only after primary is solid)

- **The man who was never taught to grieve** — 35–55, often immigrant or first-gen, often a father. Works through Faisal-style messaging.
- **The new parent navigating identity loss** — 28–40, usually mothers, postpartum + cultural translation + mother-in-law dynamics.
- **The sandwich-generation caregiver** — 40–60, parenting kids while parenting parents (often across continents).

### Who we are NOT for

- People in active mental-health crisis (route to crisis line + The Bridge).
- People looking for clinical diagnosis or treatment (route to therapy).
- People who want a chatbot (route to Replika or Pi).
- People who want a 7-day "fix."

Saying who we are not for is part of the brand. It earns trust from the people we *are* for.

## 4. Brand voice — the load-bearing rules

The voice rules below are non-negotiable. They are how a stranger knows in three sentences whether a piece of content is from Hearth or from a competitor.

### What the voice IS

- **Warm but not saccharine.** We do not perform cheerfulness.
- **Slow.** Sentences end before they should. We use white space as a value.
- **Literary.** We read like Cup of Jo, On Being, Modern Love. Not like a wellness app.
- **Specific, not abstract.** Not "we hold space" — *"the call that ruins your Sunday."*
- **Quietly authoritative.** We say what we are not. We don't apologise for it.
- **The serif speaks; the mono labels.** Fraunces is for the sentence; Inter Tight is for the supporting copy; JetBrains Mono is for the eyebrow / category / metadata.

### What the voice IS NOT

- Not therapy-influencer ("Your nervous system is BEGGING you to read this carousel 🧠✨").
- Not corporate-wellness ("At Hearth, we believe everyone deserves emotional infrastructure").
- Not bro-marketer ("STOP scrolling. This will change your life.").
- Not crisis-tourism (graphic suffering as a hook).
- Not generic ("mental health is health").

### The 7-second test

Pick up any piece of Hearth marketing. Read for 7 seconds. If a stranger could imagine *Calm, Headspace, BetterHelp, Modern Health, or Brown Girl Therapy* having written it, rewrite it.

## 5. Channel priorities

Following the platform-strategy research, three priority channels at launch and three Phase 2 channels.

### Priority channels (launch — months 0–6)

**1. Substack — the owned channel.**
- **The Hearth Letter** — a weekly literary letter from a Keeper or the founder. 600–900 words. Slow, observational, never salesy. This is the tribe's gathering place.
- Substack Notes — daily 1–2 short posts, riffing off the Letter or surfacing a single line.
- Cross-post excerpts to LinkedIn and Threads on a 24-hour delay.
- KPI: subscribers, open rate, reply rate. Target month 6: 5,000 subs, 50% open, 0.5% reply.

**2. Instagram — the visual hearth.**
- 3–5 Reels per week (whisper monologue, letter-to pattern, slow b-roll voiceover).
- 1–2 carousels per week (essay carousels — high save rate is the lever here).
- Stories daily — light, behind-the-scenes, Keeper voice notes.
- KPI: saves + DM shares (these signal real resonance, not vanity).

**3. YouTube long-form — the slow brand-building channel.**
- One 8–18 minute literary essay video per week, voiced by a Keeper or a Member, over slow b-roll.
- Format: visual essay, like *The Cinema Cartography* or *The School of Life.* Low production rate, high care.
- Doubles as cornerstone content — every Reel, Note, and carousel can be cut from this one video.
- KPI: average view duration > 50%, watch-time growth.

### Phase 2 channels (months 6–12)

**4. LinkedIn — founder-led, not brand-led.**
- The founder posts 3–5x/week from a personal account. Long-form essays, weekly cadence.
- Brand page only re-shares founder content; never originates.

**5. Threads — early-mover window closing through 2026.**
- Daily quiet posts, observational. Same voice as Substack Notes. Cross-post the same content with light tweaks.

**6. Pinterest — the overlooked channel.**
- Carousel essays converted to vertical pins. Quote cards from Embers. Idea Pins of Sit rituals. High-intent female audience that searches with quiet language.

### Channels to ignore (until proven otherwise)

- **TikTok** — 2026 mental-health moderation is hostile, brand-safety risk is real, the audience is wrong-shaped for slow literary content. Revisit when we have a Reels-tested format that's safe to port over.
- **X / Twitter** — for founder thought leadership only. The brand account does nothing here.
- **Paid Meta / Google ads on mental-health keywords** — gated, expensive, low intent. Use only retargeting (engaged Embers readers, abandoned intake) once organic engine is proven.

## 6. The atomic content engine

One piece of content gets made each week. It is then *atomized* into eleven outputs across channels.

### The atom

The atom is **one literary essay** of 800–1,200 words. Written by a Keeper, a member (with consent), or the founder. Published on Substack as the Hearth Letter on Sunday morning.

Each atom must:
- Open with a single specific sentence (no "in today's world" preambles).
- Hold one — not three — true things.
- Name a feeling people carry but don't have words for.
- End on stillness, not on a CTA. The CTA goes in the footer.

### The atomization (week-of execution)

| Day | Output | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Sun | Hearth Letter (Substack) | The atom |
| Sun pm | Substack Notes excerpt + cover line | Atom's opening sentence |
| Mon | IG carousel essay (8–10 slides) | The atom, slowed and broken |
| Mon | LinkedIn long post (founder) | The atom, reframed first-person |
| Tue | IG Reel: whisper monologue (60–75s) | The atom's central truth |
| Wed | YouTube long essay (8–18 min) | The atom, expanded with b-roll + voiceover |
| Wed | Substack Notes — single line from the YT essay | YT essay |
| Thu | IG Reel: letter-to pattern (12–20s) | One sentence from the atom |
| Thu | Threads — 3 short observational posts | Atom's marginalia |
| Fri | Pinterest pins — 3–5 quote cards | Atom's most-screenshotted lines |
| Sat | IG Reel: slow b-roll voiceover (60–90s) | Atom's closing paragraph |

This is **eleven outputs from one weekly essay.** The economics work because the atom is the only thing that requires real writing care; everything else is craft-cropping.

## 7. The funnel

Following Hormozi's value equation and Brunson's Hook-Story-Offer, but stripped of urgency-carnival mechanics.

### The funnel in plain English

```
Discovery (free content)
    ↓
Permission (Hearth Letter subscription — $0)
    ↓
Trust (3–5 letters read, an Ember saved, a Reel shared)
    ↓
Consideration (intake started)
    ↓
First Sit (paid: $39 for the month)
    ↓
Habit (Sit 2, 3, 4 — the relationship deepens)
    ↓
Upgrade or refer (Hearth Deep, or Light a Hearth gift)
```

### Conversion mechanics

- **No free trial of the membership.** A free Hearth Letter is the trial. The membership starts when they pay.
- **Soft intake first.** The 6-step intake at `/intake` is the conversion event before payment. It commits the user emotionally before they commit financially.
- **No discount-driven first month.** The $10-off exit popup is the only place we discount, and only on exit-intent. Discounts on emotional support cheapen the brand.
- **Gift as a second conversion path.** When a user won't buy for themselves, the gift framing converts them as a gifter. The exit popup uses both paths.
- **Upgrade trigger:** members on Hearthside who book an extra Sit twice in a month get a gentle email from their Keeper suggesting Hearth Deep.

### Metrics that matter (and the ones that don't)

| Metric | Why | Target by month 6 |
|---|---|---|
| Hearth Letter subscribers | Permission asset | 5,000 |
| Letter open rate | Voice resonance | 50% |
| Saves + DM shares per Reel | Resonance > vanity | Top 25% of beauty-niche reels |
| Intake completion rate | Friction signal | > 60% |
| Intake → paid conversion (30-day) | Core funnel | > 25% |
| Month-2 retention (paid) | Product-market fit | > 80% |
| Month-6 retention (paid) | True PMF | > 55% |
| NPS (members at month 3) | Word-of-mouth fuel | > 50 |
| Hearthside → Hearth Deep upgrade rate (6 mo) | Margin survival | > 40% |
| Gift purchases / month | Brand-strength signal | 50 by month 6 |

| Metrics we ignore | Why |
|---|---|
| Follower count | Vanity. Saves > followers. |
| Reach impressions | Vanity. Watch-time + completion are real. |
| Likes | Weakest signal in 2026 algorithms. |
| Daily active users | Hearth is not optimised for daily engagement. |

## 8. The brand commitments

These are commitments we make to ourselves, not the audience. The audience benefits from us keeping them.

1. **We will not run a 24-hour flash sale, ever.**
2. **We will not use the word "trauma" as marketing copy.** It belongs to clinicians and survivors, not brand decks.
3. **We will not use scrolling-stop devices that aren't honest** (no fake AI alarms, no "wait — read this before you scroll").
4. **We will not feature member faces or names without explicit signed consent + payment.**
5. **We will not run influencer campaigns with #ad disclosures buried.** Founder content is signed by the founder; sponsored content is labeled at the top, not the bottom.
6. **We will not buy follower-mentions from creators who do trauma-tourism content.**
7. **We will not optimize CAC by stripping the rituals.** The rituals are the product.
8. **We will publish the Hearth Letter every Sunday for at least 52 weeks before evaluating the channel.** Slow brand-building requires a slow timeline.

## 9. Risks and watch-list

The risks we manage actively, not just acknowledge:

- **Brené Brown / Sahaj Kohli launching a recurring membership.** Both have the brand permission and audience to enter our lane. Watch quarterly. If either launches a peer-support membership, accelerate cultural-specificity moats and Keeper density.
- **AI-companion category encroachment.** Replika, Pi, Character.ai are improving fast. Defensive content: the *"Hearth vs. AI companions"* essay published quarterly, refreshed with current data.
- **Mental-health platform moderation tightening.** Reels and TikTok could de-rank our category further. Substack and YouTube long-form are the hedge — neither is platform-beholden.
- **Stripe risk in the mental-wellness category.** Marketing language that reads "therapy adjacent" can trigger reviews. Our peer-support framing is the legal moat; protect it in copy.
- **Keeper quality erosion at scale.** Marketing demand can outpace Keeper supply. Slow growth is the safer mode here. Resist any campaign that promises capacity we don't have.

## 10. What success looks like at 12 months

- 25,000 Hearth Letter subscribers, 50% open rate
- 1,500 paying members ($75K MRR)
- 60%+ month-6 retention
- A sustainable atomic content engine producing 11 outputs/week
- One YouTube long-form video that has crossed 1M views and is the canonical introduction to Hearth
- A founder personal brand that is recognisably Hearth-aligned but separable (founder is not the only voice)
- Keeper roster of 25–40 active Keepers with 80%+ utilisation
- Two Phase-3 levers validated: (a) employer/EAP pilot, (b) one localised market beyond US/CA/UK
